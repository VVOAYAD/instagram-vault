Your body did something brilliant when it was young. It built walls. Fast. Thick. Invisible.

And those walls worked.

You survived things that should have broken you because your nervous system learned to close before the blow landed. To numb before the feeling arrived. To leave before you were left.

That was intelligence. That was love, actually — the body loving itself enough to shut down what it could not yet hold.

But here is the part no one tells you.

The war ended. And no one told your body.

So the walls stayed. The armor thickened. The fortress grew. And slowly, silently, the thing that saved your life became the thing that kept you from living it.

You wonder why you cannot feel. Why intimacy feels like threat. Why stillness feels like danger. Why love makes your chest tighten instead of open.

It is not broken. It is protected. Still.

The work is not to tear the walls down. It is to show your nervous system, slowly, in the body, that the season changed. That what was once a shield is now a cage. That the same structure serves two completely different masters depending on when you are living from.

Survival built it. Presence dissolves it. Not with force. With safety the body can finally believe.
